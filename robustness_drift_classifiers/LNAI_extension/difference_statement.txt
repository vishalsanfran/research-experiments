Statement of Differences Between the Conference Version and the Extended Version
Mona Rajhans and Vishal Khawarey

The conference paper studied adversarial robustness and SHAP-based explanation
drift for an MLP on two datasets (phishing URLs, UNSW-NB15) under FGSM and PGD,
introducing the Robustness Index (RI). This extended version adds well over 30%
new material across the following contributions.

(i) New classifier families.
Random Forest and XGBoost are evaluated alongside the MLP across all datasets and
attacks, representing the tree-ensemble family that dominates production
cybersecurity deployments.

(ii) New attack methods.
ZOO, Square Attack, and HopSkipJump extend the evaluation from white-box attacks
to a model-agnostic black-box framework applicable to non-differentiable tree
models.

(iii) New metric: Explainability Stability Index (ESI).
ESI is the complement of the normalised SHAP-drift AUC, on the same [0,1] scale
as RI. Computed via TreeSHAP for both tree models, it enables direct cross-model
comparison of explanation stability.

(iv) Two new datasets.
NF-ToN-IoT (IoT intrusion, 10 features) and HIKARI-2021 (encrypted traffic,
83 features) extend the study to four datasets across three security domains.

(v) New empirical findings.
ZOO produces near-degenerate results against XGBoost (RI ~0.98) due to
piecewise-constant prediction surfaces, while Square Attack reveals genuine
vulnerability (RI = 0.36 on Phishing) -- a 0.62 gap that depends entirely on
attack choice. A query-budget ablation confirms the degeneracy is intrinsic to
the piecewise-constant surface, not a function of query budget: XGBoost RI is
flat across all budgets on Phishing (delta = 0.000). XGBoost's high ZOO RI
coexists with low ESI (0.06-0.16 across four datasets), demonstrating that
prediction robustness and explanation stability are decoupled. The RF > XGB ESI
ordering holds under ZOO on all four datasets and under Square Attack on Phishing
and UNSW-NB15.

(vi) PGD step-size ablation.
A step-size sweep shows the PGD < FGSM anomaly from the conference paper is a
hyperparameter artefact on z-score normalised data, not a structural property of
the model.

(vii) Master comparative analysis.
A single results table spans all four datasets, three model families, and five
attack methods. Twelve new figures accompany the extended experiments.

(viii) Feature-level TreeSHAP drift.
Per-feature attribution drift heatmaps show that XGBoost's boosted-additive
structure produces consistently higher per-feature drift than RF's ensemble
averaging, which explains the lower XGBoost ESI across all datasets.

(ix) Attack-method selection guidance.
A two-axis framework (gradient dependence, query efficiency) explains the
observed attack ranking and yields practical recommendations: Square Attack as the
primary tree-ensemble benchmark; ZOO alone overestimates robustness by up to 0.62
RI; adaptive PGD step sizes for z-score normalised data.

(x) Variance study.
A five-seed study shows the ZOO degeneracy gap is reproducible (mean 0.534-0.575,
std <= 0.035). ESI variance is higher; the RF > XGB ordering holds in all five
seeds on UNSW-NB15 and in 4/5 seeds on Phishing and HIKARI-2021, but is within
noise on NF-ToN-IoT.

(xi) Adversarial training for tree ensembles.
Square Attack data augmentation raises XGBoost Square RI on Phishing from 0.372
to 0.755 (+0.383) and RF Square RI on UNSW-NB15 by +0.156. RF on Phishing
degrades slightly (-0.049), showing that augmentation epsilon should be tuned
below eps_max for already-robust models.

(xii) Limitations section.
Six scope constraints are discussed: feature-validity of L-inf perturbations in
structured security data, dataset scope, CPU-only budget, ESI approximation via
ZOO inputs, variance results, and tree-ensemble defence scope.
